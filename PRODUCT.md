# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Primary — prospective IT students** at the University of San Agustin (and nearby): first-years and shiftees deciding whether to join, checking the org out on their phone, often mid-enrollment or during org fairs. They arrive curious but unconvinced, and leave in under a minute if nothing signals "this is a real, active organization worth my time."

**Secondary — current ITSA members**: checking upcoming events, deadlines, and details. They want fast answers, not marketing.

**Tertiary — ITSA officers** (admin surface): managing events, members, and content through the Firebase-backed dashboard. Their need is speed and clarity, not persuasion.

## Product Purpose

The official web presence of the Information Technology Student Association (ITSA), University of San Agustin. It exists to:

1. **Recruit** — convert prospective students into members via the Join flow.
2. **Inform** — surface events, deadlines, and what the org actually does.
3. **Establish credibility** — prove ITSA is organized, active, and led by real people (officers), so joining feels like a smart move.

Success = a prospective student lands, believes ITSA is legitimate and active, and starts the Join flow. Secondarily, members reliably find event info without friction.

## Brand Personality

**Confident · credible · approachable.** A student org that clearly has its act together — organized, active, forward-looking — without reading as a stiff corporate body. The voice is a capable senior peer, not an administrator: direct, warm, proud of the community, never bureaucratic. It should feel unmistakably *tech* (it's an IT association) and unmistakably *student-run* — energy and humanity, backed by real structure.

Emotional goals: **trust** ("this is legit"), **belonging** ("I'd fit here"), and a quiet **pride** in the craft of the site itself — the interface is the first proof that IT students here build good things.

## Anti-references

- **Corporate / stiff intranet.** No cold enterprise dashboards, dense bureaucratic layouts, or lifeless institutional pages. Credibility must come from clarity and craft, not from looking like an HR portal.
- **Generic SaaS/AI template.** No cream-bg + tiny-uppercase-eyebrow-on-every-section + identical icon-card grids + gradient-text headings. That reads as templated and undercuts the "IT students who build good things" claim.
- **Amateur club page.** No clip art, mismatched fonts, rainbow of clashing colors, or crowded bulletin-board layouts.

## Design Principles

1. **The site is the proof.** Craft is the argument. Every interaction should quietly demonstrate that this org's members can build. Sloppiness reads as "not serious."
2. **Own the logo's identity.** The molecular/network mark (cyan→deep-blue nodes with warm orange/red accents) is the brand's real signature. Colors, motion, and shapes should descend from it — not a generic blue-purple placeholder.
3. **Credible ≠ cold.** Professionalism is delivered through structure, hierarchy, and restraint — then warmed by human touches (real officers, real events, confident copy). Never sacrifice warmth for polish or vice versa.
4. **Mobile-first, recruit-fast.** Prospective students decide on their phones in seconds. The most important thing on any screen is legible and one tap away; the Join path is never buried.
5. **Show the org is alive.** Lead with current events, real people, and activity — evidence over adjectives. Empty or stale sections actively hurt the credibility goal.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG 2.1 AA**. Body text ≥4.5:1 contrast (watch muted-gray-on-tint, the current palette's risk); large text ≥3:1. Full keyboard operability for nav, forms, and the Join flow. Visible focus states. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` with meaningful non-motion fallbacks. Don't rely on color alone to convey state (the blue/orange brand split must not be the only signal). Readable on low-end phones over slow campus connections.
